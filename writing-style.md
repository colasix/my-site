# WebXC Writing Style & Tone Guide

This guide defines how WebXC communicates across all digital channels. Every piece of copy should feel like it comes from James or Andy: confident practitioners who've built real digital assets for real businesses.

---

## Voice

Write in second person ("you") when addressing the reader and first person plural ("we") when speaking as WebXC. The reader should feel spoken to by a business partner, not a vendor.

Never hedge. Say "we build" not "we help you build" or "we aim to deliver." Say "you'll see" not "you may see." Certainty is the baseline. This voice states, it doesn't suggest.

Contractions are standard and expected ("we're," "you'll," "it's," "that's," "don't," "you've"). Avoid them only in moments of deliberate weight or precision.

---

## Sentence Structure & Rhythm

Default to short, declarative sentences. One idea per sentence. Let full stops do the work.

Mix punchy 3-8 word sentences with occasional longer sentences (max ~25 words) for explanation or context. The ratio should lean heavily toward short. This mirrors how experienced operators think: direct, then clarifying.

Use parallel structure and repetition as a rhetorical device. Mirror sentence shapes to build rhythm and conviction.

Example (what this looks like):
- "Your site performs. Your content ranks. Your leads convert."
- "We don't build websites. We build conversion engines. Schema markup, entity architecture, and every technical layer configured for AI readability."

---

## Tone

Confident but not arrogant. Authority comes through methodology, demonstrated results, and deep expertise. Never through empty superlatives or self-congratulation. Let specifics do the convincing.

Warm but direct. You're speaking to business owners and directors who've already built something. Respect that. Be friendly, not corporate. Approachable, not casual to the point of being unserious.

The calm of someone who's already done this hundreds of times. No panic. No hype. Just competence.

---

## The Bilingual Voice (WebXC)

WebXC operates fluently in both business strategy language and technical digital language. Your copy should reflect this bilingual competence.

**Strategy vocabulary:** positioning, commercial logic, customer journey, conversion architecture, trust signals, differentiation, competitive moat, decision drivers, proof points, authority markers.

**Technical vocabulary:** schema markup, entity resolution, semantic architecture, Core Web Vitals, AI crawlability, topical authority, entity recognition, semantic HTML, structured data, canonical resolution.

When both registers appear together, they should feel like fluency, not code-switching. You're not switching between two languages. You're speaking as someone who understands both domains completely.

Example:
"Your positioning defines what we build. We then encode that positioning into schema markup so search engines and AI models understand it the same way you do. Entity resolution across your entire digital footprint ensures consistent recognition."

This isn't "strategy language plus technical language." It's integrated thinking.

---

## Word Choice

Plain language only. No jargon, no marketing fluff, no buzzwords. If a word sounds like it came from a corporate slide deck, cut it.

Favour concrete, specific words over abstract ones.

- Write "90+ PageSpeed score" not "high performance"
- Write "30 core entities mapped and validated" not "comprehensive digital foundation"
- Write "We configure structured data for every product, review, and article" not "We optimise your semantic architecture"

Avoid entirely: "leverage," "synergy," "holistic," "bespoke," "solution" (as a standalone noun), "utilize," "cutting-edge," "next-level," "game-changer," "empower," "innovative," "best-in-class," "world-class," "seamless."

Avoid almost entirely: "that said," "in fact," "essentially," "arguably," "quite," "fairly," "rather."

---

## Hard Rules (Non-Negotiable)

Never use em dashes. Use commas, full stops, colons, or restructure the sentence instead. This is a hard rule with no exceptions.

Avoid exclamation marks almost entirely. Confidence doesn't shout. Use them only for genuine surprise or emphasis, and very sparingly.

NZ spelling throughout: optimise, programme, colour, centre, behaviour, analyse, organisation, digitalise, customise.

No colons in headings. Use full stops or rephrase.

Active voice exclusively. Always. No exceptions.

## Two Operational Modes

WebXC operates in two distinct communication modes. Both are you. Both are confident. The register shifts with context.

**Advisory Mode (Discovery, Strategy, Workshops, Positioning Refinement)**

You're the calm expert who's seen this pattern dozens of times. You're asking good questions and synthesising the business owner's thinking. You're helping them see what matters.

Tone: measured, consultative, patient. Authority through clarity, not speed.

Example:
"Let's look at where your customers actually make decisions. Most business owners think it happens on the homepage. Usually it doesn't. For your market, it happens in the third or fourth page of consideration, usually at 11pm when the director is researching options alone."

**Delivery Mode (Build, Implementation, Technical Execution, Performance Reports)**

You're direct and assertive. You know what you're building, why, and how. You're confident in the work and the outcomes.

Tone: precise, certain, commanding. Authority through expertise and results.

Example:
"We've structured your product schema to pass Google's Merchant Center validation. We've mapped fifty-three entities to Wikidata for recognition consistency. Your Core Web Vitals are now 92, 98, 100. Your organic traffic will move week one."

---

## Who This Voice Is NOT

Not a digital agency voice. No hype. No "crushing it." No startup energy. No "we're disrupting the industry."

Not corporate or formal. No boardroom language. No passive voice. No management speak.

Not salesy or high-pressure. No urgency tactics. No artificial scarcity. No "limited time" language.

Not vague or hedging. Not "we aim to help you explore possibilities." Not "we believe in digital transformation." Be specific. Be certain.

Not purely technical. Never reduce a business to metrics without honouring what the owner actually built. You're talking to the builder, not the spreadsheet.

---

## The Quick Checklist

Before any copy goes live, ask:

Could James and Andy both read this aloud and mean it without wincing?

Are sentences averaging under 15 words? If not, break them up.

Are both registers present? Does someone hear both the business strategist and the technical builder?

Is there any empty adjective without methodology or data behind it? Cut it.

Did you avoid em dashes completely?

Read it aloud. Does it sound like a calm, authoritative specialist talking? Like a business partner who understands both strategy and execution? Good. Does it sound like a brochure? Rewrite.

## Examples of This Voice in Action

**What we write:**

"Your site is built for two audiences. Google's systems. And human buyers. Most sites prioritise one. We optimise for both simultaneously. Schema markup that's technically flawless. Copy that speaks directly to your customer's commercial logic. When they align, conversion follows."

**What we don't write:**

"We utilise cutting-edge semantic architecture and best-in-class conversion optimisation strategies to empower your business and drive transformational results through innovative digital solutions."

(Every word here is corporate fluff masking an absence of specificity.)

**What we write:**

"Forty years of combined experience taught us one thing: you can't out-market a broken product. You also can't out-perform a poorly positioned one. We start with positioning. We verify it works commercially. Then we build the technical architecture that makes it visible to the right buyers at the right moment."

**What we don't write:**

"Our holistic approach leverages synergistic methodologies to create a seamless customer journey experience across all touchpoints, maximising engagement and driving business growth."

(This says nothing. It could describe anything. It describes nothing.)

## Entity Resolution & Tone Consistency

Every piece of copy about WebXC is a WebXC entity. Consistency matters. This guide is your master reference for how that entity speaks.

When in doubt, ask: "Would a founder building a real digital performance business for other real businesses say this? Or would a marketing manager say it?"

If it's the latter, rewrite.
